# Utah Sociolinguistic Interview Protocol

## Study Overview
This study investigates the role of **lexical surprisal** in conditioning patterns of **/t/-glottalization** in Utahn English. Because /t/-glottalization is socially stigmatized, we examine whether speakers engage in **hypercorrection** (realizing /t/ as [t]) in high-surprisal contexts and **hypocorrection** (glottal stop or creaky voice) in low-surprisal contexts. These patterns are compared against a non-stigmatized regional feature, the **FAIL–FELL merger** to [fɛl], which serves as a dialectal baseline.

The session consists of a **Labovian sociolinguistic interview** followed by a **sentence-reading task**. The interview is structured to gradually shift from low-monitoring to higher-monitoring speech styles.

---

## Interview Structure (Approx. 35–45 minutes)

### Section 1: Warm-Up & Rapport Building (5 minutes)
**Goal:** Establish comfort and conversational rhythm; minimize self-consciousness.

**Prompt examples:**
- "What year were you born?"
- "Where did you grow up?”
- "Can you tell me what cities you have lived in and for how long?"
- "Do you speak any other languages and what is your fluency?"
- "What does a typical day look like for you right now?”

**Interviewer notes:**
- Avoid evaluative feedback.
- Use minimal encouragers ("mm-hmm," "yeah").
- Do not mention speech or language.

---

### Section 2: Positive Personal Narratives (10–15 minutes)
**Goal:** Elicit highly involved speech with low self-monitoring and low lexical surprisal.

#### 2a. Pride & Achievement Narratives examples
- "What’s something you’re really proud of accomplishing?”
- "Can you tell me about a time when something worked out better than you expected?”
- "What’s a challenge you’re glad you took on?”

#### 2b. Place-Based & Identity Narratives examples
- "What’s your favorite place in Utah, and what makes it special to you?”
- "Are there any Utah traditions or events you especially enjoy?”
- "What would a perfect weekend here look like for you?”

#### 2c. Humor & Storytelling examples
- "Can you tell me a funny story that you or your family likes to tell?”
- "Have you ever had a funny misunderstanding or mix-up?”

**Interviewer notes:**
- Allow long turns; do not interrupt.
- Follow up with neutral prompts ("What happened next?", "How did that turn out?").
- Avoid steering toward emotional extremes.

---

### Section 3: Expertise & Procedural Talk (5–10 minutes)
**Goal:** Sustain fluent, confident speech with reduced monitoring.

**Prompt examples:**
- "Is there something you’re really good at or know a lot about?”
- "Can you walk me through how someone would learn to do that?”
- "What’s the biggest mistake beginners usually make?”

**Interviewer notes:**
- Let participants take an expert role.
- Encourage explanation rather than evaluation.

---

### Section 4: Transition to Reading Task (2–3 minutes)
**Goal:** Explicitly shift to careful speech style.

**Scripted transition:**
> "Thanks for sharing all of that. The last part of the session is a short reading task. I’ll give you some sentences to read aloud at a comfortable pace. If you feel like you made a mistake please wait a second or so and then reread the sentence.”

---

## Sentence-Reading Task (Separate Protocol)
Participants complete a controlled sentence-reading task designed to manipulate **lexical surprisal** and target environments for:
- /t/-glottalization vs. /t/-strengthening
- FAIL–FELL merger contexts
- filler words to act as page buffers

---

## Rationale for Interview Design
Positive personal narratives were selected to promote speaker involvement and reduce attention to speech, consistent with Labovian methods for eliciting vernacular styles. This approach avoids reliance on narratives of danger or distress while maintaining the sociolinguistic conditions necessary to observe stigmatized and non-stigmatized variable usage.

The progression from casual conversation to explicit reading tasks allows for systematic comparison across speech styles differing in social awareness and surprisal.

## Summary of Expected Style Shifting

|           Component          | Monitoring |          Surprisal         |                Expected outcome                |
|:----------------------------:|:----------:|:--------------------------:|:----------------------------------------------:|
| Warm-up 			  		   |     Low 	| Low					     | Baseline vernacular 							  |
| Interview narratives         |     Low    | Low                        | Hypocorrection ↑ (glottal stop / creaky voice) |
| Opinion talk                 |   Medium   | Medium                     | Mixed realization ([t] ~ [ʔ])                  |
| Expertise talk          |    High    | Medium                     | Hypercorrection ↑ ([t])                        |
| Reading task                 |    High    | Experimentally manipulated | Increasing surprisal → hypercorrection ↑       |
| FAIL–FELL (control variable) |     All    | All                        | Minimal change across conditions               |
